The Career of the Notorious RBG

Bri Corley

In 1993, Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg was appointed to the Supreme Court of the United States by President Bill Clinton after serving on the U.S. Court of Appeals for 13 years prior.


In her 27 years on the Supreme Court, she became a vocal advocate for the rights of women, worker's rights, and the seperation of church and state. In 1999, she was awarded the American Bar Association's Thurgood Marshall Award for her contributions to civil rights

As a justice who was unafraid to speak her mind, she wrote strong opinions on cases that ranged from same-sex marriage to the Virginia Military Institutes ban on female cadets and more hardhitting issues.



As we look back on her life, let's take a moment to read some of the deepest words on some of the largest cases of her Supreme Court career






The Cases

The data and stats behind some of RBGs landmark cases.

  • United States v Virginia (1996)
  • Question: Should female cadets be allowed to enter the Virginia Military Institute or would a womens'-only academy be equal to VMI?

  • Argued: January 17th, 1996
  • Decided: June 26th, 1996
  • Ruling: VMI's male-only admissions policy lacked an "exceedingly persuasive justification" violating the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment

  • 7-1

    In favor of the United States
  • Obergefeel v Hodges
    (2015)
  • Question: Can marriage licenses be granted to same-sex couples and be recognized on a federal level?

  • Argued: April 28th, 2015
  • Decided: June 26th, 2015
  • Ruling: The Fourteenth Amendment requires both marriage licensing and recognition for same-sex couples

  • 5-4

    In favor of Obergefell
  • Olmstead v L.C.
    (1999)
  • Question: Should financial constraints determine whether states comply by the ADA guidelines concerning community treatment programs for the mentally disabled?
  • Argued: April 21st, 1999
  • Decided: June 22nd, 1999
  • Ruling: No, but financial constraints might be significant if the state can show that allocation of resources to one patient will cause harm to others.

  • 6-3

    In favor of L.C.
The Impact from then to now

United States v Virginia (1996)

Since the ruling in 1996, over 431 female cadets have graduated from VMI. In 2017, the rarely public Justice Ginsburg even went to speak to graduating cadets and she is often credited with helping change the institution as a whole.



Obergefell v Hodges
(2015)

This case essentially shut down any marriage discrimination laws and made all states recognize same-sex marriage licenses from other states, which was a big win for the LBGTQ+ community. It is often cited as one of the most influential Supreme Court cases of recent history.

Olmstead v L.C.
(1999)

As a result of the Olmstead decision, many individuals with mental disabilities have transitioned from an institution to the community, and many individuals have avoided unnecessary institutionalization. It is one of the biggest advancements in equal rights for people with disabilities since the ADA was passed in 1990.
